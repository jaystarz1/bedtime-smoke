Goodnight, Hush Hollow

In the Wood, where the trees stand tall and the moss grows deep, Bunny and Hedgehog found a little wooden cart beside the path. It had one long handle, smooth and worn.

One handle. Two friends.

"I want to pull it," said Bunny.

"I want to pull it," said Hedgehog.

So the cart sat still, and nobody moved, and the Wood went quiet and dim.

Then along came Bear, slow and heavy, his fur brushing the ferns. He set down one big paw and rumbled, low as a log rolling over.

"One handle," said Bear. "Two friends. So take it turn by turn."

And that is what they did.

Bunny held the handle first and went ten slow steps down the path. Hedgehog walked beside, counting soft. Then Hedgehog held the handle and went ten slow steps, and Bunny walked beside, counting soft.

Your turn, my turn, soft and slow.

They gathered pinecones as they went and set them in the cart, one and one and one. Ten steps, and ten steps, and ten steps more. Nobody hurried. Nobody had to.

The cart grew heavy. The sky went from gold to grey to blue.

Your turn, my turn, soft and slow.

Bear came behind them all the way, humming a rumble with no words in it, and the ferns leaned aside to let the little cart by.

At the top of the last small hill, the handle was heavy for one. So they held it together, one hand and one hand, and brought the cart down into Hush Hollow side by side.

Your turn, my turn, soft and slow.

They stacked the pinecones by the door, one and one and one, until the stack stood neat and round and done. Then they sat on the step, tired and glad, with the good ache of a finished job in their arms.

"That was a big job," said Bunny.

"That was a slow job," said Hedgehog.

"That," said Bear, "was a job for two."

The Hollow grew soft and warm. Somewhere a cricket kept a small steady beat. Bunny yawned. Hedgehog yawned. Bear sat down and closed his eyes, and his rumble went quieter, and quieter, and quiet.

Goodnight, Bunny.

Goodnight, Hedgehog.

Goodnight, Bear.

Goodnight, moon.

Goodnight, Hush Hollow.
