The Basket at the Cove

Down in the Cove, where the water sings soft and slow, Mouse and Bunny sat close on a warm flat stone. Between them lay a little basket of sweet moon-berries, glowing gold in the dark.

The tide lay low and calm. Evening settled soft over the water, and the two of them sat warm and near, as close as close could be.

"If we share them," said Mouse, "there will be none left for us."

Bunny held the basket close. "Only a few," she whispered. "Only these few."

But along the shore came the small ones of the Cove, a sleepy line of them, quiet and near. And Wink, the little glow of the Cove, drifted down and settled on the basket's rim, his light warm and round.

Mouse turned to Bunny. Bunny turned to Mouse.

So they shared. One berry, then another, passed from hand to hand around the stone. One for you, and one for you, and one for the smallest of all. And the softest, strangest thing began, for every berry given away, another glowed up gold in the basket. More and more, soft and slow.

They gave, and the basket filled again. More and more, soft and slow.

Wink glimmered brighter. The small ones leaned in near. Around and around the berries went, warm from hand to hand. No one went without, and still there was plenty, the basket warmer for every kindness, a little kindness, soft and small.

When the last of the small ones had eaten their fill, Mouse and Bunny carried the basket home along the quiet shore, up the path to the Hollow, where the beds were soft and the night was kind.

They lay down warm all through, the good warmth of a shared thing coming back round. More and more, soft and slow.

Goodnight, Mouse. Goodnight, Bunny. Goodnight, little Wink, glowing low. Goodnight, moon, high and quiet over the Cove.

Goodnight, Hush Hollow.
